# Sprint Retrospectives

## Definition

The Sprint Retrospective is the Scrum event where the team inspects itself and creates a plan for improvement. The team examines how the last Sprint went with regard to individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and the Definition of Done, then identifies the most helpful changes to make.

The Sprint Retrospective concludes the Sprint and is time-boxed to a maximum of three hours for a one-month Sprint, with shorter Sprints proportionally shorter.

Core questions:

* What went well during the Sprint?
* What problems did we encounter?
* How were (or weren't) those problems solved?
* What will we do differently next Sprint?

The most important output of a retrospective is a small set of concrete, actionable improvement items — typically one to three — with clear owners, reviewed at the start of the next retrospective.

---

## Why Retrospectives Matter

The retrospective is the engine of continuous improvement (kaizen) in Agile. Without regular reflection and adaptation, teams repeat the same mistakes and processes ossify. The twelfth Agile principle states: at regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

## Popular Retrospective Formats

Popular retrospective formats include Start-Stop-Continue, the Four Ls, Sailboat, Mad-Sad-Glad, Timeline, and Starfish. Varying the format keeps retrospectives engaging and surfaces different kinds of insight.

### Start, Stop, Continue

The team answers three questions: What should we start doing? What should we stop doing? What should we continue doing? Simple, fast, and action-oriented.

### The Four Ls (4Ls)

The team collects items under four headings: Liked, Learned, Lacked, and Longed For. Good for balancing positives with gaps.

### Sailboat (or Speedboat)

The team draws a sailboat. Wind in the sails represents what pushes the team forward; anchors represent what holds the team back; rocks represent risks ahead; the island represents the goal. Visual and good for mixed groups.

### Mad, Sad, Glad

Team members place items under Mad, Sad, or Glad to explore the emotional experience of the Sprint. Useful when morale or conflict is the real topic.

### Timeline Retrospective

The team builds a chronological timeline of the Sprint, marking events as positive or negative. Good for long Sprints or after incidents, because it grounds discussion in facts.

### Starfish

Five categories: Keep Doing, Less Of, More Of, Stop Doing, Start Doing. A finer-grained variant of Start-Stop-Continue.

## Structure of an Effective Retrospective

A well-known five-stage structure (from "Agile Retrospectives" by Derby and Larsen):

1. Set the stage: establish focus and psychological safety.
2. Gather data: collect facts and feelings about the Sprint.
3. Generate insights: look for patterns and root causes.
4. Decide what to do: choose one to three concrete improvement actions with owners.
5. Close the retrospective: summarize and appreciate contributions.

Techniques for root cause analysis during the insight stage include the Five Whys and fishbone (Ishikawa) diagrams.

---

## Psychological Safety

Retrospectives only work when people can speak honestly without fear of blame. The Prime Directive (Norm Kerth) sets the tone: "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand."

Facilitation practices that protect safety:

* Focus on systems and processes, not individuals.
* Keep what is said in the room confidential unless agreed otherwise.
* Managers attend only if the team invites them.
* Rotate facilitation or use a neutral facilitator for charged topics.

## Retrospective Anti-Patterns

### No Action Items

The most common retrospective anti-pattern: the team talks but commits to no action items, so no improvement happens and the retrospective becomes waste. Continuous improvement requires concrete action. Fix: end every retrospective with one to three owned, achievable actions and review them next time.

### Same Issues Every Sprint

Recurring complaints with no progress signal that action items are too vague, lack owners, or address symptoms instead of root causes.

### Blame Game

Retrospectives that assign fault to individuals destroy psychological safety; people stop raising real problems and the meeting becomes theater.

### Skipping Retrospectives

Teams under pressure often cut the retrospective first — exactly when reflection is most needed. Skipping retrospectives abandons continuous improvement.

### Retrospective as Status Meeting

Reviewing ticket-by-ticket progress belongs in other events. The retrospective inspects how the team works, not what the team worked on.

### Only Negative Focus

Ignoring what went well demoralizes the team and loses the chance to amplify successful practices.

### Action Item Overload

Committing to ten improvements means completing none. Limit to one to three high-impact actions per Sprint.

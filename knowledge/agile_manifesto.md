# Agile Manifesto

## Definition

Agile is a mindset and set of principles for delivering value through iterative development, customer collaboration, continuous learning, and adaptation to change.

Agile is not a framework, methodology, process, or tool.

Common frameworks that implement Agile principles include:

* Scrum
* Kanban
* Extreme Programming (XP)
* Lean Software Development

---

## Purpose

Agile exists to:

* Deliver customer value early and continuously.
* Reduce risk through frequent feedback.
* Adapt to changing requirements.
* Improve collaboration between business and technical teams.
* Enable continuous improvement.

---

## The Four Values

The four values of the Agile Manifesto are:

1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
4. Responding to change over following a plan

While there is value in the items on the right, Agile values the items on the left more.

### 1. Individuals and Interactions Over Processes and Tools

Preference:

People, communication, collaboration, and relationships.

Not:

Rigid adherence to processes, tools, or bureaucracy.

Implications:

* Communication is prioritized over documentation handoffs.
* Teams are empowered to solve problems.
* Processes support people, not the reverse.

---

### 2. Working Software Over Comprehensive Documentation

Preference:

Delivering usable outcomes.

Not:

Producing excessive documentation without delivering value.

Implications:

* Documentation should provide value.
* Progress is measured through working outcomes.
* Delivery is prioritized over paperwork.

---

### 3. Customer Collaboration Over Contract Negotiation

Preference:

Continuous engagement with customers and stakeholders.

Not:

Relying solely on predefined agreements.

Implications:

* Feedback is frequent.
* Priorities can evolve.
* Product direction adapts to learning.

---

### 4. Responding to Change Over Following a Plan

Preference:

Adaptation based on new information.

Not:

Blind adherence to outdated plans.

Implications:

* Plans are useful but not fixed.
* Learning drives decisions.
* Change is treated as an opportunity.

---

## Value Interpretation Rule

The items on the right retain value.

Agile does not reject:

* Processes
* Tools
* Documentation
* Contracts
* Planning

Agile values the items on the left more when trade-offs occur.

---

## The Twelve Principles

The twelve principles of the Agile Manifesto are: customer satisfaction through early and continuous delivery, welcoming changing requirements, delivering working software frequently, daily collaboration between business and developers, building projects around motivated individuals, face-to-face conversation, working software as the primary measure of progress, sustainable pace, technical excellence, simplicity, self-organizing teams, and regular reflection and adaptation.

### 1. Customer Satisfaction Through Continuous Delivery

Objective:

Deliver valuable outcomes early and continuously.

Indicators:

* Frequent releases
* Customer feedback
* Measurable business value

---

### 2. Welcome Changing Requirements

Objective:

Use change as a competitive advantage.

Implications:

* Requirements may evolve.
* Teams remain adaptable.
* Backlogs remain dynamic.

---

### 3. Deliver Frequently

Objective:

Reduce feedback cycles.

Recommended:

* Weeks rather than months.
* Smaller increments rather than large releases.

Benefits:

* Faster learning
* Reduced risk
* Earlier value realization

---

### 4. Business and Development Collaborate Daily

Objective:

Reduce misunderstanding and decision latency.

Practices:

* Shared planning
* Frequent stakeholder engagement
* Joint prioritization

---

### 5. Build Around Motivated Individuals

Objective:

Enable ownership and accountability.

Requirements:

* Trust
* Autonomy
* Support
* Psychological safety

---

### 6. Prefer Direct Communication

Objective:

Improve clarity and speed of information exchange.

Modern Interpretation:

* Face-to-face when possible.
* Real-time communication when distributed.
* Minimize communication delays.

---

### 7. Working Software Is the Primary Measure of Progress

Objective:

Focus on outcomes rather than activity.

Examples of poor progress indicators:

* Hours worked
* Documents created
* Tasks started

Examples of strong progress indicators:

* Working features
* Customer value delivered
* Production outcomes

---

### 8. Maintain Sustainable Pace

Objective:

Enable long-term effectiveness.

Characteristics:

* Predictable delivery
* Healthy workload
* Reduced burnout
* Consistent quality

---

### 9. Technical Excellence Enhances Agility

Objective:

Maintain the ability to change.

Examples:

* Automated testing
* Continuous integration
* Refactoring
* Clean architecture
* Code quality practices

Consequences of neglect:

* Technical debt
* Reduced velocity
* Increased defects

---

### 10. Simplicity Is Essential

Definition:

Maximizing the amount of work not done.

Objective:

Deliver the simplest solution that achieves the desired outcome.

Practices:

* Avoid over-engineering.
* Eliminate unnecessary complexity.
* Focus on essential value.

---

### 11. Self-Managing Teams Produce Better Solutions

Objective:

Leverage collective expertise.

Characteristics:

* Shared ownership
* Distributed decision-making
* Cross-functional collaboration

Benefits:

* Better architectures
* Better designs
* Faster problem solving

---

### 12. Regular Reflection and Adaptation

Objective:

Continuously improve effectiveness.

Practices:

* Retrospectives
* Lessons learned
* Process experiments
* Continuous improvement initiatives

Cycle:

Inspect -> Learn -> Adapt

---

## Core Agile Concepts

### Iterative Development

Work is delivered in small increments and improved over time.

### Incremental Delivery

Each delivery adds usable value.

### Empiricism

Decisions are based on observation, feedback, and evidence.

### Continuous Improvement

Teams continuously inspect and adapt their processes.

### Customer-Centricity

Customer outcomes drive prioritization and decision-making.

### Quality Built In

Quality is part of development, not a separate phase.

---

## Common Agile Anti-Patterns

### Agile Without Customer Feedback

Problem:

Iterations occur without validating value.

Result:

Fast delivery of the wrong solution.

---

### Agile as Micromanagement

Problem:

Daily tracking replaces team autonomy.

Result:

Reduced ownership and innovation.

---

### Agile Without Technical Excellence

Problem:

Speed is prioritized over quality.

Result:

Accumulating technical debt.

---

### Agile as "No Planning"

Problem:

Teams stop planning altogether.

Result:

Chaos and unpredictability.

Reality:

Agile promotes adaptive planning, not the absence of planning.

---

### Agile as Scrum Ceremonies Only

Problem:

Events are performed without embracing Agile principles.

Result:

Mechanical process with limited value.

---

## Success Criteria

An Agile organization demonstrates:

* Frequent value delivery
* Strong customer collaboration
* Rapid feedback loops
* Adaptability to change
* High transparency
* Continuous improvement
* Sustainable pace
* Technical excellence
* Empowered teams
* Outcome-focused decision making

---

## Relationship to Scrum and Kanban

Agile is the mindset.

Scrum and Kanban are frameworks and methods that help teams apply Agile principles.

Hierarchy:

Agile Manifesto
├── Agile Principles
├── Scrum
├── Kanban
├── Extreme Programming (XP)
└── Lean Practices
